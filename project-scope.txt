Chapter 1: Project Description
1.1 Motivation and Background
For as long as firearms have been used for militaries, personal defense, hunting, and recreation,
training with them has been detrimental to their effectiveness in every application. Firearm
training traditionally uses live ammunition. Live ammunition, whether it be black powder and
ball, or modern day cartridges, each is expensive and requires facility capable of safely stopping
bullets, and as well as safely managing the smoke and fumes from the gunpowder. For these
reasons, a firearm training system that doesn’t require live ammunition and a range, would make
firearm training cheaper and more accessible.

Our solution is to develop a comprehensive laser based training system, rather than just a
standalone target. The user shoots a laser at the target, which will detect and show where the
laser hit. By pairing the target with a dedicated mobile application, this system will allow users
to train, track comprehensive statistics, and improve their aim without needing to go to the range.

In addition to reducing the cost of training, the system is intended to improve the quality of
practice by providing immediate feedback after every shot. Traditional paper targets require
shooters to stop their training session and walk downrange to inspect their results, disrupting the
flow of practice and making it more difficult to recognize shooting patterns. A laser-based
electronic target can instantly display shot placement, allowing users to make corrections in real
time while maintaining a consistent training rhythm. By pairing this instant feedback with stored
performance data, shooters can monitor long-term improvement, identify recurring errors, and
objectively evaluate their progress over multiple training sessions.

Another important consideration is accessibility. Not all firearm owners have convenient access
to a shooting range, and many indoor or outdoor ranges have limitations on operating hours,
travel distance, or usage fees. A portable laser training system allows users to practice
fundamental shooting skills such as sight alignment, trigger control, and target transitions in a
safe environment without the logistical challenges associated with live-fire training. This makes
regular practice more practical while reinforcing safe firearm handling habits outside of the
range.

1.2 Existing Products and Prior Related Work
Various laser training systems have been developed in the past. One being the LaserLyte
TLB-LMS Steel Tyme system [1]. This product uses a proprietary laser pistol, and a target that
lights up and plays a sound when hit. Another product is the Mantis BlackbeardX Dry Fire Laser
Training System [2]. This uses a laser system that replaces the magazine and bolt of an AR-15,
and connects to the user’s phone to track where the shots land on the target. The camera of the
user’s phone will record where each laser hit on the target. Many other laser target systems have
been made that simply show whether the shot was a hit or a miss, in other words, if the laser shot
was detected anywhere on the target it would be registered as a hit.

Although these products have proven that laser-based firearm training is both practical and
effective, they also demonstrate several limitations. Many systems only indicate whether a target
has been hit rather than determining the precise impact location. Others rely on a smartphone
camera to detect laser strikes, making performance dependent on camera placement, lighting
conditions, and image processing algorithms. Additionally, several commercial products require
proprietary laser cartridges, dedicated training firearms, or subscription-based software,
increasing the overall cost and reducing compatibility with different firearm platforms.

The PrecisionShot Training System is designed to address many of these limitations by
performing shot detection directly on the target using a dense array of phototransistors. Instead of
depending on an external camera, the embedded hardware determines the location of each laser
strike and immediately displays the result through an integrated LED array. This approach
reduces external dependencies while improving response time and allowing the system to operate
as a standalone device. The addition of a companion mobile application further expands the
system by providing training history, performance statistics, configurable training modes, and
wireless control without sacrificing standalone functionality.


1.3 Project Goals
The main goal of this project is to develop a target system that will detect and show placement of
a laser shot. Additionally different settings and modes will be implemented into the target so that
the user will have various training options. The target should also be able to be used outside in
daylight, and will run off of a rechargeable battery.

1.4 Project Objectives
The objectives of the PrecisionShot are as follows:

    ●​ Develop an array of phototransistors that detect incoming laser shots and not any other
       light
    ●​ Implement an LED array to indicate where the laser shot was detected within 10ms
    ●​ Program a microcontroller to process the phototransistor detection, LED indication, and
       different settings and modes
    ●​ Show accurate shot placement in both indoor and outdoor lighting conditions within
       0.1cm
    ●​ The app must update within 1 second of the laser hitting the target

These objectives define the measurable performance goals for the PrecisionShot Training
System. The system must not only detect a laser hit, but also determine the location of that hit
with enough accuracy to provide useful training feedback. This requires the phototransistor array,
LED feedback system, embedded software, and mobile application to work together as one
complete system. The 10 ms LED response requirement ensures that physical feedback appears
nearly instantly, while the 1 second app update requirement ensures that wireless data
transmission remains fast enough for a smooth training experience.

Because the system is intended to function in both indoor and outdoor environments, the shot
detection process must be designed to separate valid laser strikes from unwanted ambient light.
The microcontroller will be responsible for reading the sensor array, filtering the input signals,
determining the shot location, updating the LED array, and sending the shot data to the mobile
application. These objectives provide the foundation for later hardware, software, and testing
requirements.
1.5 Features and Functionalities
The PrecisionShot Training System combines embedded hardware with wireless software to
provide a complete firearm training platform. While the target itself is capable of operating
independently, integration with the companion mobile application provides additional
functionality such as long-term performance tracking, configurable training modes, and remote
control of system settings. Together, these features provide users with immediate visual feedback
while collecting meaningful training data that can be used to evaluate accuracy and monitor
improvement over time.

   ●​ The user will be able to change the settings of the target for different modes.
   ●​ The target will work wirelessly with an internal rechargeable battery.
   ●​ The system features full wireless app integration for logging training data, user stats, and
      session history.
   ●​ The target will be able to be used indoors and outdoors.
   ●​ The target shows real-time shot placement on the physical target and on the app.

These features make the PrecisionShot system more than a simple hit-detection target. The
physical target provides immediate local feedback through the LED array and onboard controls,
while the mobile application expands the system into a training and performance-tracking tool.
This allows users to review previous sessions, monitor accuracy trends, and adjust training
settings without relying only on the physical target interface.

The combination of wireless operation, rechargeable power, real-time feedback, and mobile app
integration supports the overall goal of creating a portable and accessible laser training system.
Since the device can operate indoors or outdoors and does not require a wired connection during
normal use, it can be used in a variety of training environments. This flexibility helps make the
system practical for repeated use while still supporting more advanced software features through
the companion application.

1.6 Design Requirements
The design of the PrecisionShot Training System is driven by the need to provide fast, accurate,
and reliable laser shot detection under a variety of operating conditions. The phototransistor
array must be capable of detecting the selected laser wavelength while rejecting ambient light
sources such as indoor lighting and sunlight. This requires both carefully selected sensor
hardware and software-based threshold calibration to minimize false detections while
maintaining high sensitivity to valid laser shots. The LED array must also provide nearly
instantaneous visual feedback so users can immediately identify shot placement without
interrupting their training session.

The embedded microcontroller must coordinate communication between all major hardware
subsystems, including the phototransistor array, LED array, display, user controls, wireless
communication module, and external storage. Processing must occur with sufficiently low
latency to maintain real-time system performance while simultaneously supporting multiple
training modes, user settings, and wireless communication with the companion mobile
application. The firmware must also remain flexible enough to accommodate future software
updates and additional training features.
Portability is another important design consideration. The complete system must operate
wirelessly from a rechargeable battery while providing sufficient runtime for extended training
sessions. Wireless Bluetooth communication should allow the companion application to receive
shot information, display user statistics, and remotely adjust target settings without affecting the
target's ability to function independently. Finally, the physical enclosure must protect the
electronic components while remaining lightweight, durable, and suitable for repeated indoor
and outdoor use.

These design requirements connect the project objectives to the actual engineering choices that
must be made during development. Sensor spacing, signal filtering, microcontroller selection,
wireless communication, battery capacity, PCB routing, and enclosure design all affect whether
the final system can meet its accuracy, speed, and portability goals. Each design choice must
therefore support the larger requirement of building a system that is reliable, easy to use, and
capable of giving meaningful training feedback in real time.

The system must also be designed with testing and future refinement in mind. Since the final
prototype will include several interacting subsystems, the hardware and software should be
organized so that individual components can be tested separately before full integration. This
includes verifying sensor detection, LED response, wireless communication, display output,
battery operation, and enclosure alignment. Designing for testability will reduce debugging time
and improve the likelihood that the final prototype performs consistently during demonstration.

Chapter 2: Engineering Specifications
2.1 System-Level Engineering Specifications
2.1.1 Overall System Specifications
The PrecisionShot Training System is designed to provide a portable, standalone laser firearm
training platform capable of accurately detecting laser impacts, providing immediate visual
feedback, and recording training performance for later analysis. The system combines embedded
hardware with a companion mobile application to create an affordable alternative to live-fire
practice while maintaining realistic training conditions. Unlike many commercial laser training
systems that depend on external cameras or internet connectivity, PrecisionShot performs all shot
detection directly within the target, allowing it to operate independently without requiring
additional equipment.

The system is intended primarily for indoor use with commercially available laser training
cartridges or laser training devices that emit visible laser pulses. The sensing system must detect
these laser pulses with sufficient sensitivity to accurately determine shot location while rejecting
interference from normal ambient lighting. Because indoor lighting conditions may vary
significantly between environments, the detection system must also support adjustable sensitivity
through software calibration to maintain reliable performance under different operating
conditions.

To successfully manage these environmental constraints, the physical design relies on a
mechanical grid mesh faceplate integrated directly over the phototransistor matrix. Ambient
indoor light, particularly overhead fluorescent or LED fixtures, can introduce significant
background noise that risks saturating the optical sensors and masking short laser pulses. The
geometric structure of the front-facing grid addresses this vulnerability by acting as a directional
light filter, introducing physical isolation that restricts light capture exclusively to perpendicular,
front-angled paths. By creating deep, angled cell walls that block oblique, off-axis ambient light
from the sides, the faceplate establishes a controlled internal testing environment directly above
the sensor lenses. This hardware isolation minimizes background interference before it reaches
the electronics, lowering the baseline noise floor and allowing the firmware to consistently
differentiate true laser impacts from static room lighting.

Real time operation is a fundamental requirement of the project. After a laser strike is detected,
the system must process sensor information, estimate the impact location, calculate the
appropriate score, and provide immediate visual feedback with minimal delay. Fast system
response improves the training experience by allowing users to instantly identify shot placement
and make corrective adjustments without interrupting practice.

The PrecisionShot target is designed to function as a complete standalone training device. Users
should be able to operate the system directly from the target through an onboard display and
physical controls without requiring a smartphone connection. Essential functions, including
training mode selection, score display, distance selection, calibration adjustment, and battery
monitoring, must remain available regardless of whether the companion application is connected.

Wireless communication provides additional functionality without becoming a dependency for
normal operation. Bluetooth Low Energy communication allows the companion mobile
application to receive shot information, store training history, display performance statistics, and
remotely configure target settings. Since all primary processing occurs within the embedded
hardware, temporary communication interruptions do not prevent the target from continuing
normal operation.

Portability is another major design objective. The entire system must operate from an internal
rechargeable battery while providing sufficient operating time for extended practice sessions.
Power consumption must therefore be minimized through efficient hardware selection,
event-driven communication, and intelligent firmware design. The physical enclosure must also
protect internal electronics while remaining lightweight enough to be easily transported between
training locations.

Finally, the system architecture must remain scalable for future improvements. The hardware and
software should allow additional sensors, training modes, user profiles, communication features,
and advanced analytics to be incorporated without requiring significant redesign of the overall
platform. This modular approach extends the useful lifetime of the project while simplifying
future development.

2.2 Component Specifications
2.2.1 Component Overview
The PrecisionShot Training System consists of several major hardware and software subsystems
that work together to provide accurate shot detection, real-time processing, user feedback, and
long-term performance analysis. Each subsystem has its own functional requirements that
contribute to the overall performance and reliability of the system. The following sections
describe the primary responsibilities and performance requirements of each subsystem without
specifying the exact components selected for implementation.

2.2.2 Phototransistor Detection System
The sensing subsystem serves as the primary input mechanism for the PrecisionShot Training
System. Its purpose is to detect incoming laser pulses across the target surface and convert the
received optical energy into electrical signals that can be processed by the embedded controller.
Since shot location accuracy depends directly on sensor performance, the detection system must
provide consistent, repeatable measurements while minimizing false detections caused by
ambient lighting.

The sensor array must provide sufficient spatial resolution to accurately estimate the location of
each laser impact. Rather than simply detecting whether the target has been struck, the system
must collect enough information to estimate the position of the laser strike with significantly
greater precision than the spacing between individual sensors. The sensing subsystem must also
respond quickly enough to detect short laser pulses generated by commercially available training
cartridges.

The detection system must remain reliable under a variety of indoor lighting conditions.
Software-adjustable detection thresholds should allow users to compensate for different lighting
environments while maintaining sensitivity to valid laser impacts. The overall sensor architecture
should also remain scalable so that future revisions of the project can increase sensing resolution
without fundamentally changing the operating principles of the system.

2.2.3 Signal Multiplexing System
The sensing subsystem requires a method of reading a large number of analog sensor outputs
using a limited number of microcontroller input channels. The multiplexing subsystem is
responsible for sequentially routing sensor signals to the analog-to-digital converter while
preserving the analog voltage information required for accurate shot location calculations.

The multiplexing architecture must support rapid scanning of the complete sensor array while
introducing minimal signal distortion or electrical interference. Since sensor measurements are
used to estimate laser position, the subsystem must preserve relative signal amplitudes instead of
reducing each sensor output to a simple digital state. The design should also remain expandable
so additional sensing channels can be incorporated without significantly increasing processing
complexity or printed circuit board size.

2.2.4 Processing System
The processing subsystem coordinates the operation of every major component within the
PrecisionShot Training System. It must continuously acquire sensor measurements, execute the
shot detection algorithm, calculate impact location, determine scoring information, update user
interfaces, and manage wireless communication while maintaining real-time system
responsiveness.

The processor must provide sufficient computational performance to execute these tasks
simultaneously without introducing noticeable delays during operation. It must also provide
adequate memory, analog input capability, digital communication interfaces, and expansion
resources to support both the current prototype and future feature development. The processing
subsystem should remain capable of standalone operation even when external communication
devices are unavailable.

2.2.5 Visual Feedback System
The visual feedback subsystem provides immediate confirmation of each detected laser impact.
Once the processing subsystem determines the estimated shot location, the feedback system must
clearly indicate the corresponding impact position on the target while simultaneously presenting
important system information to the user.

The visual interface should provide sufficient brightness for indoor operation while maintaining
low power consumption during battery-powered use. In addition to displaying shot location, the
subsystem should support multiple colors or visual patterns for future training modes, system
notifications, and diagnostic functions. Immediate visual response is essential for maintaining a
realistic and uninterrupted training experience.

2.2.6 User Interface System
The user interface allows the PrecisionShot target to function independently of the companion
mobile application. Physical controls and an onboard display provide users with direct access to
essential operating functions, including training mode selection, calibration adjustment, score
display, battery status, and system configuration.

The interface must remain intuitive and responsive while minimizing the number of user
interactions required during normal operation. Users should be able to configure the target
quickly without interrupting training sessions, and all essential settings should remain accessible
even when wireless communication is unavailable.

2.2.7 Wireless Communication System
Wireless communication enables data exchange between the embedded target and the companion
mobile application. The communication subsystem must reliably transmit processed shot
information while also receiving user commands and configuration updates from the mobile
application.

The communication protocol should prioritize low power consumption, reliable operation, and
minimal latency. Since the target performs all critical processing locally, wireless communication
should enhance the user experience without becoming necessary for basic functionality.
Temporary communication interruptions should not affect shot detection or normal target
operation.

2.2.8 Power Management System
The power subsystem supplies stable operating voltages to every electronic component within
the PrecisionShot Training System. Since the device is intended to operate from a rechargeable
battery, power management must maximize operating time while maintaining stable electrical
performance under varying load conditions.
Voltage regulation must provide clean, reliable power for both analog sensing circuitry and
digital processing hardware. The subsystem should also monitor battery condition and provide
users with battery status information to prevent unexpected shutdown during training sessions.
Protection against overcharging, excessive discharge, and electrical faults should be incorporated
to improve long-term reliability and user safety.

2.2.9 Companion Mobile Application
The companion mobile application extends the functionality of the PrecisionShot target by
providing long-term data storage, performance analysis, remote configuration, and system
diagnostics. Although the target is designed to function independently, the application provides
users with a more comprehensive view of their training progress over time.

The application should maintain a local history of shot data, calculate performance metrics, and
present graphical summaries that help users evaluate improvements in accuracy and consistency.
It should also allow remote modification of target settings without requiring users to physically
access the target during training. All application data should remain available without requiring
cloud storage or internet connectivity, ensuring both user privacy and continued operation
regardless of network availability.

3.2 Sensor and Feedback System
3.2.1 Sensor Array Design
The sensor array is one of the most critical aspects of the entire system, it is the mechanism that
determines when and where a given shot lands on the target face. Depending on the exact
placement of a shot, an x,y coordinate can be calculated by averaging the voltage readings of the
sensors activated in a cluster. Phototransistors conduct electricity proportionally to the light
detected, so particular sensors will receive higher readings than ones further from the actual
location the shot was placed.

The 400 phototransistors will fit on the target in an ellipse shape, further broken down into
"zones" which will vary in density and distance from the bullseye.

3.2.2 Phototransistor Testing
In order to accurately detect the wavelength of red to be fired at the target, we need to choose a
phototransistor with a spectral response that peaks around 630 nm to 680 nm. The part we
decided on for testing is the ALS-PT204-6C/L177 630nm radial phototransistor. Our primary
reasoning for using this over other options is the spectral response, which peaks at 630nm and
rejects IR, ideal for testing our red laser. We considered a handful of other through-hole
phototransistors, below is a table of alternatives as well as the component we decided on.

laser itself this may require careful threshold calibration.

choose an SMD equivalent part for the final board.

Our current option for the SMD version of the component is the ALS-PT19-315C/L177/TR8, as
it has a nearly identical spectral response. However there are other considerations for which

be discussed further for approval.

3.2.4 Visual Feedback Feedback Target Array
To enhance user interaction and provide immediate feedback, the target board features a
localized visual indicator system. Each of the 400 individual phototransistor sensing cells is
paired with a dedicated surface-mount Light Emitting Diode (LED). When a laser strike breaks
the detection threshold of a specific phototransistor, its adjacent LED illuminates instantly. This
provides a direct real-time visual confirmation of the hit across the target face without requiring
the user to look away or check the primary 5-inch TFT LCD panel.

From a hardware implementation standpoint, the visual feedback loop can be achieved through
two primary routing configurations: direct hardware driving or software-multiplexed signaling.In
a direct hardware configuration, each phototransistor acts as a local switch, using a small-signal
transistor or a dedicated buffer to route power straight to its paired LED when exposed to light.
This creates zero software overhead and a near-instantaneous response time, though it demands
careful current-limiting design to prevent excessive power drops across a large active sensor
matrix.
Alternatively, in a software-multiplexed approach, the ESP32-S3 microcontroller registers the
matrix scan hit and commands a dedicated shift-register chain or LED matrix driver IC to
illuminate the corresponding coordinate. This software-driven path allows for advanced
telemetry features, such as sustaining the LED illumination for a fixed, user-defined window or
flashing specific patterns to indicate high-accuracy zone hits.

The layout of these components requires precision to prevent mechanical and optical
interference. To ensure a clear, un-compromised target zone, each LED is placed closely next to
its paired phototransistor beneath a 3D-printed grid matrix. Each grid cell contains an isolated
sensor-emitter pair separated by a thin, opaque plastic internal wall. This prevents internal light
bleed, ensuring that a glowing LED does not accidentally trigger its own phototransistor or
adjacent cells.

Furthermore, to ensure long-term durability against repeated vibrations and drops, all visual
indicators use an 0805 surface-mount package format. This choice provides clear high-intensity
visibility through the target mesh while completely adhering to the project's manufacturing
guidelines, which prohibit any micro-passives smaller than the standard 0805 footprint.

3.2.5 Multiplexing Architecture
Our phototransistor array needs to feed signals into the ESP32 in order to calculate shot
placement. However, our MCU module can only interface with a certain number of GPIO pins,
thus limiting how many devices can communicate with it. So how exactly can we interface the
ESP32 with 400 phototransistors with 20 ADC pins? Our solution is multiplexing, multiplexers
can converge several analog signals into one channel using a digital switch.

An analog multiplexer is simple in concept: it is a digitally-controlled rotary switch. A 16
channel multiplexer can detect 16 separate inputs using 4 digital select pins, 4 select pins mean
we can assign a 4-bit binary address to each phototransistor. What this means is that we can pass
the analog voltage reading of the phototransistor output through the mux, depending on the
channel assigned the 4-bit select pins can identify which phototransistor the signal came from.
Then we pass this voltage reading to the ADC converter of the ESP32, which can digitize that
reading and pass it on to the MCU for further processing.

The design choice we need to make is how we implement these multiplexers to interface with the
ADC converters, we have two viable options here. Either we can run a single stage of muxes
directly to the ADC pins, or we utilize a tiered system of muxes (routing each mux into another
set of muxes), each having benefits and drawbacks. The first option restricts our GPIO
significantly, utilizing all available ADC pinouts on our ESP32 and requiring some to handle
several multiplexers. The other route allows us to save on GPIO at the cost of speed and an
increase in series resistance, potentially creating signal degradation.

3.2.6 MUX Selection
In the example above we mentioned using a 16-channel multiplexer, however several other
options present as viable alternatives. In this section, we will compare different multiplexers and
what criteria are most important in selecting the one for our application.
ADG732 - a monolithic CMOS 32-channel analog mux. This instead uses a 5-bit binary address
to control the switching, more input channels means less multiplexers and output channels
overall. It has superior electrical specifications over the 16-channel options, offering low "ON"
resistance which helps preserve signal integrity. The biggest caveat here is cost, each 32-channel
mux is significantly more expensive while only doing the job of two 16-channel muxes.

ADG1606 - a monolithic iCMOS 16-channel analog mux. Same 16-channel design as described
earlier, with 4-bit binary address lines. The important advantage this has over other muxes is its
incredibly low RON which stays flat across the low voltage range of inputs it will be reading. This
means for any given signal, regardless of the voltage, the resistance across the switch will remain
the same. In terms of signal preservation, the ADG1606 is our strongest option; if the error
introduced by other muxes inferiorly degrades our input. It is more expensive because of this
reason, however.

74HC4067 - a CMOS 16-channel analog mux. This mux in particular is ubiquitous, which means
it's well-documented and has existing libraries and example code. Architecturally
interchangeable for the ADG1606, the same 4-bit address means we can swap these for another
16-channel option if necessary. It has far superior switching speed than the 1606, which allows
us to budget for a higher latency elsewhere. It is also considerably cheaper than the previous
options presented. This comes with the drawback of a non-linear RON which instead varies
across different input voltages.

3.3 Processing and Control System
3.3.1 MCU Module
The microcontroller we choose for our system must support all communication technologies and
be able to interface with each hardware device. Since we plan to have an app communicate with
the MCU, Wi-Fi and Bluetooth capabilities are non-negotiable. Another important consideration
is power draw. The MCU will be operational as long as the device is powered, so we need to
minimize current draw to avoid reducing battery life. Given these restrictions, we landed on the
ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8, satisfying both the communication and power requirements.

The ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8 is well suited for this project because it combines processing,
memory, wireless communication, and hardware interfaces into a single compact module. The
module is built around the ESP32-S3 series SoC, which includes a dual-core 32-bit Xtensa LX7
microprocessor capable of operating up to 240 MHz. This gives the system enough processing
capability to handle sensor scanning, shot detection, scoring logic, display updates, Bluetooth
communication, and debug output without requiring a separate processor. The selected N8R8
variant also includes 8 MB of flash and 8 MB of PSRAM, giving the firmware more room for
program storage, buffers, display graphics, Bluetooth packet handling, and future software
expansion.

The module also supports a large number of useful embedded peripherals for the PrecisionShot
system. These include GPIO, ADC, SPI, I2C, UART, LCD interface support, LED PWM, USB
Serial/JTAG, SD/MMC host support, timers, and watchdogs. This is important because the MCU
must communicate with many different subsystems, including the sensor multiplexer network,
TFT display, physical buttons, SD card storage, LED feedback hardware, and the wireless
mobile application. The ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8 also operates from a 3.0 V to 3.6 V
supply, which matches the planned 3.3 V system rail and makes it compatible with the rest of the
low-voltage digital hardware.

3.3.2 Microcontroller Selection
Our original MCU was planned to be the ESP32-WROOM-32E. This has since shifted to the
ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8, which more neatly fits our requirements. The most notable
advantage is the Bluetooth capability. The N8R8 supports BLE, or Bluetooth Low Energy,
instead of relying on Bluetooth Classic. Bluetooth Classic was never an important aspect of the
MCU because our design does not require continuous audio-style signal output. Our use case
will benefit more from the high bandwidth options and lower power operation available through
BLE.

The ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8 also provides a stronger development path than the original
ESP32-WROOM-32E option. Since PrecisionShot requires real-time sensor processing and app
communication, the selected module gives the team more room to grow as the firmware becomes
more complex. The extra PSRAM is especially useful for buffering sensor readings, handling
display elements, storing temporary shot data, and supporting future debug tools. This makes the
S3 module a better fit for a system that is expected to handle embedded control, wireless
communication, and local user feedback at the same time.

Another major reason for selecting the ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8 is its built-in USB
Serial/JTAG controller. This allows the team to program and debug the module more directly
during development, reducing the need for extra USB-to-UART bridge circuitry on the final
PCB. This simplifies the control board design, lowers component count, and makes firmware
testing easier during prototyping. Since Senior Design II will require repeated firmware updates,
debugging, and hardware testing, this built-in development support reduces design risk and
makes the module more practical for the team’s timeline.

3.3.3 Programming and Debug
As mentioned previously, the onboard JTAG controller allows us to easily program the
microcontroller. There are other important considerations for how we interface with the MCU,
such as the boot and strapping switches.

The ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8 includes a USB Serial/JTAG controller, which simplifies
firmware development because the team can program, monitor, and debug the microcontroller
without adding a separate USB-to-UART bridge circuit. This will be useful during prototype
testing because the firmware will need frequent updates as the sensor array, multiplexer
scanning, display output, Bluetooth communication, and calibration logic are developed. The
boot and enable pins must also be handled correctly in the PCB design so the device can reliably
enter normal boot mode, enter download mode when needed, and recover during debugging if
firmware or hardware issues occur.

3.4 Display and Physical User Interface
3.4.1 Display Requirements
An important part of the PrecisionShot user interface is the onboard display. The display must
provide consistent real-time feedback so the user can see system status, active mode, shot
location, score, calibration information, and other relevant training data without relying entirely
on the mobile application. Since the target is designed to operate as a standalone device, the
display must be clear enough to support normal use even when the app is not connected.

For this application, the graphical output must be detailed enough to show a rendered target area
with visible shot placements from a reasonable distance. The display should be readable indoors
and outdoors, while also keeping power consumption low enough to support battery-powered
operation. It must also interface cleanly with the selected ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8
microcontroller without requiring unnecessary support hardware. Cost, size, color output, refresh
rate, and ease of software integration are also important factors because the display must fit
within the enclosure and remain practical for the project budget.

Several display technologies were considered for the onboard user interface. Each option has
advantages and disadvantages related to cost, power draw, readability, graphical capability, and
ESP32 compatibility. The following section compares the most relevant display types and
explains why a TFT LCD is the best fit for this system.

3.4.2 Display Technology Comparison
Character LCD - A character LCD is a monochrome display that uses fixed text cells. It is
simple, inexpensive, and easy to program, but it does not meet the graphical needs of the
PrecisionShot system. Since it can only display letters, numbers, and symbols, it cannot show a
rendered target or precise shot placement. This makes it unsuitable for the onboard visual
feedback required by this project.

Passive Graphic LCD - A passive graphic LCD uses a monochrome electrode matrix to display
basic graphics. This type of display is attractive because it is low cost and has very low power
consumption. However, passive graphic LCDs usually have lower refresh rates, limited contrast,
and lower resolutions. These limitations make them a poor fit for displaying active shot
feedback, especially when the system may need to show many possible shot locations across the
target area.
Vacuum Fluorescent Display - A vacuum fluorescent display is a bright monochrome display
that uses cathodoluminescence. It offers strong visibility, including in bright environments, but it
is not ideal for this project. VFDs are typically better suited for text, numbers, and simple
indicators rather than detailed graphical target rendering. They also have higher power
consumption than other display options, which makes them less practical for a battery-powered
training device.

OLED - An OLED display uses self-illuminating pixels and provides excellent image quality,
high contrast, true blacks, and strong color reproduction. These characteristics would create a
high-quality user experience, but larger color OLED displays are expensive compared to other
options. Smaller or monochrome OLED modules are more affordable, but they do not provide
the size or graphical capability needed for this system. OLED displays may also be more
vulnerable to long-term wear or burn-in, especially if static interface elements remain on screen
for extended periods.

TFT LCD - A TFT LCD is an active matrix display that uses a transistor and capacitor at each
pixel. This technology supports full-color graphics, comes in a wide range of sizes, and is much
more affordable than larger OLED displays. TFT LCDs also avoid OLED burn-in concerns and
are commonly used in embedded systems. The main drawbacks are backlight power
consumption, lower contrast than OLED, and narrower viewing angles depending on the panel
type. Even with these limitations, TFT LCD technology provides the best overall balance of cost,
graphical capability, size, availability, and ESP32 compatibility for the PrecisionShot system.

3.5 PCB Design
3.5.1 PCB Architecture
The PrecisionShot PCB architecture is centered around separating the high-density sensor array
from the control, power, and user interface sections while still keeping all subsystems electrically
connected through a single organized board design. The lower target region of the PCB will
contain the phototransistor array, LED feedback components, and related signal routing, while
the upper control region will contain the ESP32-S3 module, display connections, buttons, power
input, voltage regulation, and debugging access. This layout keeps the sensor area aligned with
the physical target faceplate while allowing the control electronics to remain accessible for
testing and assembly.




Figure 3.5: Main PCB Architecture and Subsystem Connections

3.5.2 Sensor Array PCB
The sensor array PCB houses the complete phototransistor matrix responsible for capturing and
localizing incoming laser strikes across the target face. To achieve the resolution necessary for
precise training telemetry, the board is configured as a dense, uniform grid of 28 columns by 20
rows, yielding 560 total physical positions. To optimize processing efficiency and align with the
target's active capture zone, exactly 400 of these positions are populated with active
phototransistor sensors, following the mapped spatial layout of the target system. Each populated
sensor corresponds directly to a known coordinate pair on the board, allowing the central
firmware to map real-time analog or digital voltage transitions directly to precise physical shot
coordinates.

To manage the high volume of sensor lines without creating a bottleneck at the central
microcontroller, the layout is organized into repeatable, modular sensor banks. These banks are
routed in localized clusters that interface directly with the system's distributed multiplexer
architecture, keeping individual trace runs short and balancing the signal load across the bus.
Trace routing prioritizes strict geometric symmetry and consistent spacing to ensure uniform
signal propagation and to minimize cross-talk between adjacent sensor channels.

Furthermore, the component placement on the PCB is strictly bound to the mechanical
dimensions of the front-facing grid plate. Every phototransistor is centered precisely beneath the
4.5 mm by 4.5 mm aperture shelves at the base of the chamfered grid cells. This precise
mechanical-to-electrical alignment ensures that each sensor sits perfectly within the shadow of
its respective 3 mm vertical well, isolating the active optical components from overlapping light
paths and guaranteeing that each node only registers strikes from its designated target location..

3.5.3 Control and Interface Connections
The control and interface section of the PCB will connect the ESP32-S3 microcontroller to the
major hardware subsystems, including the sensor multiplexers, TFT display, physical buttons,
SD card interface, wireless module functions, and optional support sensors. SPI connections will
be reserved for higher-speed devices such as the display and possible SD card module, while I2C
may be used for lower-speed devices such as the touch controller or ambient light sensor. Digital
GPIO lines will be used for multiplexer select pins, enable lines, buttons, reset functions, and
other control signals. These connections should be clearly labeled in the schematic and PCB
layout to reduce assembly mistakes during testing.

3.5.4 Power Distribution and Routing
The PCB power system will receive a 5 V input from the selected commercial power bank and
distribute that rail to any components that require 5 V operation. The onboard voltage regulation
stage will then generate a stable 3.3 V rail for the ESP32-S3, sensor circuitry, multiplexers, logic
components, and other low-voltage devices. Power traces should be sized appropriately for the
expected current draw, and local decoupling capacitors should be placed near the ESP32-S3,
multiplexer banks, display connector, and voltage regulator. Analog sensor routing should be
kept away from noisy power and LED switching paths where possible to reduce interference in
the phototransistor readings.

3.5.5 Debugging and Test Points
The PCB should include accessible debugging points so the team can verify electrical behavior
during assembly and bring-up. Test points should be added for the main 5 V input rail, regulated
3.3 V rail, ground, regulator output, selected multiplexer outputs, SPI signals, I2C signals, boot
pins, reset/enable lines, and important ESP32-S3 GPIO connections. These test points will allow
the team to use a multimeter, oscilloscope, or logic analyzer to confirm power stability, sensor
output behavior, multiplexer addressing, display communication, and firmware-controlled
signals. Adding these features early will make troubleshooting significantly easier once the board
is assembled.

3.6.3 Voltage Regulation
The original plan was to have two separate voltage regulators for 3.3V and 5V rails that our
system will require. Due to the fact a commercial power bank with an internal power IC will be
utilized that can output 5V, this removes the need for a boost regulator entirely.

However, the 3.3V rail still leaves us with the decision of having a buck regulator or low
drop-out regulator, each having their own benefits and drawbacks. The core logic blocks
including the raw ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N8R8 module, the 5-inch TFT LCD, and the
400-sensor multiplexer array grids demand a highly stable, low-noise operating voltage. To
fulfill the explicit requirement for an onboard switching regulator, the main board layout
integrates a synchronous Step-Down (Buck) Switching Regulator reference design (utilizing an
industry-standard TLV62569 or AP63203 buck converter IC) directly into the copper trace
schematic.
The custom buck stage replaces standard linear LDOs to drastically maximize power efficiency
and mitigate thermal buildup within the sealed enclosure.

Once a synchronous buck converter topology was selected, several commercially available
regulators were evaluated to determine the most suitable device for the PrecisionShot Training
System. Selection criteria included input voltage range, maximum output current, conversion
efficiency, package style, PCB layout complexity, thermal performance, component availability,
and manufacturer support. Since this regulator serves as the primary power source for nearly
every subsystem within the project, reliability and ease of implementation were considered just
as important as electrical performance.

The Texas Instruments TLV62569 was initially considered due to its excellent efficiency and
compact implementation. The device accepts input voltages from 2.5 V to 5.5 V, making it well
suited for single-cell lithium-ion battery applications, and is capable of supplying up to 2 A of
continuous output current. Its switching frequency of approximately 1.5 MHz allows the use of
relatively small external inductors and capacitors, reducing the overall PCB footprint. However,
the regulator is only available in a 2 mm × 2 mm SOT563/DSBGA package, which has
extremely fine lead spacing and, in some versions, solder connections located entirely beneath
the package. These package styles are intended primarily for automated assembly and are
significantly more difficult to hand solder, inspect, and rework during prototype development.
Although electrically capable of meeting the project requirements, the TLV62569 was eliminated
because its miniature package increased manufacturing complexity without providing a
substantial performance advantage.

The Diodes Incorporated AP63203 was also evaluated as a potential solution. This synchronous
buck regulator supports a wide 3.8 V to 32 V input voltage range, allowing considerable
flexibility for future power source changes, while providing up to 3 A of continuous output
current. Typical conversion efficiencies exceed 90% across much of its operating range, and the
device integrates both switching MOSFETs, reducing the required external component count.
The AP63203 is available in a TSOT-26 package, which includes exposed side leads and is
significantly easier to assemble than BGA-style devices. Despite these advantages,
documentation, reference layouts, and application support were less comprehensive than those
provided by Texas Instruments. Since this project requires a custom PCB and extensive
prototype testing, the availability of detailed layout recommendations and validated reference
designs was considered an important factor in reducing design risk.

Older asynchronous buck regulators, including devices such as the LM2596, were also reviewed.
The LM2596 has been widely used in power supply applications for many years and supports
input voltages up to 40 V while supplying as much as 3 A of output current. However, the
regulator operates at a relatively low switching frequency of approximately 150 kHz, requiring a
much larger external inductor and larger filter capacitors than more modern designs. In addition,
the asynchronous architecture relies on an external Schottky diode, introducing additional
conduction losses that reduce efficiency and increase heat generation. The large TO-263 package
and increased PCB area required by the external components made the LM2596 less desirable
for a compact, battery-powered embedded system.
Linear regulators such as the AMS1117-3.3 were also briefly considered because of their
simplicity and extremely low component count. The AMS1117 accepts input voltages up to 15 V
and can supply approximately 1 A of output current under appropriate thermal conditions.
Packaged in a readily solderable SOT-223 package, it requires only input and output bypass
capacitors to operate. However, because the device dissipates the voltage difference between the
input and output as heat, supplying approximately 1 A from a 5 V input to a 3.3 V output would
result in nearly 1.7 W of continuous power dissipation. This would significantly reduce battery
operating time while requiring considerably more thermal management than a switching
regulator. For these reasons, linear regulation was determined to be unsuitable for powering the
primary system rail.

After evaluating the available options, the Texas Instruments LMR33620ADDA was selected as
the voltage regulator for the PrecisionShot Training System. The device operates over an input
voltage range of 3.8 V to 36 V, comfortably accommodating the regulated 5 V supplied by the
USB-C power bank while providing flexibility for future power supply modifications. It is
capable of delivering up to 2 A of continuous output current, providing significant margin above
the estimated system load and allowing additional peripherals to be incorporated in future
revisions without redesigning the power subsystem.

Unlike many compact regulators intended for consumer electronics, the LMR33620ADDA is
packaged in an 8-pin SO PowerPAD package with exposed side leads, making it substantially
easier to assemble, inspect, and rework during prototype development than BGA or leadless
packages. The integrated thermal pad also provides an efficient path for heat dissipation when
connected to the PCB ground plane using thermal vias. Texas Instruments further supports the
device with comprehensive application notes, PCB layout guidelines, simulation models,
WEBENCH design tools, and multiple validated reference designs. These resources significantly
reduce design uncertainty while improving the likelihood of first-pass PCB success.

The LMR33620ADDA also incorporates several integrated protection and monitoring features,
including cycle-by-cycle current limiting, thermal shutdown, under-voltage lockout, soft-start
functionality, and an open-drain Power Good (PG) output. The PG output may be connected to a
spare ESP32-S3 GPIO through a pull-up resistor, allowing the firmware to verify that the 3.3 V
rail has reached regulation before initializing the remainder of the system. This additional
monitoring capability improves overall system robustness while requiring minimal additional
hardware.

Considering its electrical performance, thermal efficiency, package style, manufacturability, and
extensive design support, the LMR33620ADDA provided the best overall balance of
performance and reliability for the PrecisionShot Training System and was therefore selected as
the primary voltage regulator.

3.6.4 Battery Monitoring
A typical cell-voltage fuel gauge IC is structurally incompatible with our design, as the
commercial power bank operates as a sealed module that obscures direct access to its internal
lithium cells. While the power bank features a built-in physical charge indicator, relying entirely
on this hardware component provides the central microcontroller with no direct visibility into the
state-of-charge or SOC telemetry, preventing the system from displaying live power metrics on
the integrated user dashboard.

To overcome this visibility constraint without altering the hardware of the power bank, the
system architecture utilizes a load-side Coulomb counting methodology implemented via the
Texas Instruments INA219 high-side power monitor. Positioned directly across the input 5V rail
on the main PCB, the INA219 measures current flow by tracking the voltage drop across an
onboard, precision low-side shunt resistor. Rather than relying on simple, volatile
voltage-to-capacity lookup curves which fluctuate heavily under immediate system current
spikes when the 400-sensor matrix or feedback LEDs cycle, the firmware continuously integrates
these current measurements over discrete time intervals.

This mathematical integration allows the system to accurately track the exact accumulation of
milliampere-hours entering and exiting the device. By calculating this real-time telemetry stream
against the known 5000 mAh total capacity baseline, the system can project a highly accurate,
un-shifting state-of-charge percentage directly onto the TFT display panel, providing a
completely standalone battery monitoring dashboard independent of any companion software
applications.

3.6.5 Power Safety and Protection Mechanisms
To mitigate electrical risks during operational deployment and replenishment cycles, the power
architecture incorporates multiple redundant safety layers. Because the semi-solid-state cells are
housed inside a commercial power bank module, primary battery safety is regulated by an
integrated battery management system internal to the battery casing. This integrated hardware
controller monitors the cell pack for overvoltage, undervoltage, and thermal thresholds,
terminating the charge or discharge path if any single parameter shifts outside safe operational
bounds. On the custom PCB layer, overcurrent protection is established at the primary 5V input
node using a surface-mount resettable polyfuse. If a short circuit or an unexpected component
failure occurs anywhere down-rail such as within the LMR33620 buck converter stage or the
extensive 400-sensor matrix the sharp rise in current causes the fuse to rapidly transition into a
high-resistance state. This effectively isolates the sensitive mainboard electronics from the power
source before any permanent hardware damage or hazardous thermal escalation can take place.

The way the battery is physically handled and mounted is also carefully planned to prevent
accidental damage. The power bank sits tightly inside a custom bracket that is 3D printed
directly into the target frame, separating the battery from loose wires or sharp screws. This
bracket is lined with rubber strips to create a tight friction fit, which stops the battery from
sliding around and protects the semi-solid-state gel inside from bumps and drops during
transport. To prevent unauthorized or accidental access to the internal electronics, the enclosure
is completely sealed. Reaching the battery or the main PCB requires the removal of recessed hex
screws located on the rear panel of the chassis. By leaving out any external clips or latches, the
design ensures that the only part of the power system a user ever interacts with is the external
USB-C charging port.

Chapter 4: Software Design
4.1 Software Architecture
4.1.1 Software Overview
The software for the PrecisionShot Training System is responsible for controlling the main
behavior of the target, processing laser shot data, calculating scoring information, updating the
physical display, handling user input, managing Bluetooth communication, and supporting the
optional mobile application. The software design is split into two main parts: the embedded
software running on the ESP32-S3 inside the target and the mobile application running on the
user’s phone. The target itself is treated as the main system, while the phone app is treated as a
companion tool for remote control, analytics, logging, and history.

The embedded software will be written in C++ using the ESP-IDF development framework in
Visual Studio Code. This approach was selected because the ESP32-S3 needs to handle
time-sensitive tasks such as reading the phototransistor sensor array, scanning sensor groups
through multiplexers, filtering false triggers, calculating shot location, updating the display, and
sending Bluetooth data. Since these tasks are close to the hardware and need to respond quickly,
the firmware must be reliable and organized around the real-time behavior of the device.

The basic software loop starts when the target is powered on. The ESP32 initializes the display,
buttons or rotary controls, Bluetooth communication, calibration settings, local logging system,
and sensor input system. After startup, the target enters a ready state where it waits for user input
or a laser shot. When a shot is detected by the phototransistor array, the software reads the
triggered sensor data, applies the current threshold setting, removes weak or distant noise,
calculates the estimated shot location, determines the score based on the active mode, updates the
display, and logs the event. If the mobile app is connected, the target also sends the shot
information to the app through Bluetooth Low Energy.

The target is designed to work fully without the mobile app. Without the app, the user can still
power on the system, change modes, adjust the detection threshold, enter or select distance
settings, shoot at the target, and view results on the built-in display. In this standalone mode, the
target provides immediate feedback such as shot location, score, current mode, calibration value,
and basic system status. This is important because the PrecisionShot system should still function
as a complete training device even if the user does not have a phone connected.

The mobile application will be developed using React Native with Expo and TypeScript. The app
will connect directly to the ESP32 through Bluetooth Low Energy and will not require Wi-Fi,
cloud storage, user accounts, or an internet connection. The app’s main purpose is to make the
system easier to control from a distance and to provide features that are better suited for a phone
screen. These features include viewing training history, logging shot data, changing modes,
adjusting distance, changing calibration settings, displaying analytics, and showing debug
information when needed.

The software design also separates normal user behavior from debugging behavior. In normal
operation, the system only needs to send and store the final shot result, such as the shot location,
score, mode, distance, and timestamp. In debug mode, the system can send more detailed
information such as active sensor IDs, threshold values, calibration data, and raw or filtered
sensor readings. This allows the team to test and tune the shot detection algorithm without
overwhelming the normal user interface.

Overall, the software architecture follows a device-first design. The ESP32 handles the core
training system and keeps the target usable at all times, while the mobile app adds convenience,
remote control, history, analytics, and debugging tools. This split allows the system to stay
reliable as a standalone target while still providing the extra features needed to make
PrecisionShot feel like a complete training platform.

4.2.1 Microcontroller Software Responsibilities
The microcontroller is responsible for the real-time software behavior inside the PrecisionShot

4.2.2 Sensor Processing and Multiplexer Architecture
The phototransistor array will most likely be read in groups using multiplexers. Since the target
may contain a large number of sensors, the ESP32 cannot treat every sensor as a simple
dedicated input without additional hardware support. Multiplexers allow the system to scan
groups of sensors and determine which sensor or group of sensors detected the laser.

Each sensor will need a known ID and a known physical location on the target grid. The
firmware will use this mapping to convert sensor readings into a position on the target. When a
sensor is triggered, the software can look up where that sensor is located and use that information
to calculate the estimated shot location.

The sensor processing system will compare readings against the global threshold value. Sensors
below the threshold will be ignored, while sensors above the threshold will be treated as possible
shot candidates. The software will then identify the strongest triggered sensor and check nearby
sensors to form a valid shot cluster.
The final shot location will be calculated using a weighted centroid method. Stronger sensor
readings will have more influence on the final estimated position, while weaker nearby readings
can still help improve accuracy. Distant weak readings will be ignored as likely noise or
reflections, and distant strong clusters may be treated as multiple shots.

4.2.3 Shot Detection Logic
The shot detection logic is responsible for determining when a valid laser shot has hit the target
and estimating where that shot landed. This is one of the most important parts of the
PrecisionShot software because almost every other feature depends on it. Scoring, display
feedback, Bluetooth data, local logging, and app analytics all depend on the system being able to
correctly detect and locate a shot.

The target will use a phototransistor sensor array arranged across the target face. Each sensor
will have a known position in the target grid, such as an x and y coordinate or a row and column
location. When the laser hits the target, one or more phototransistors will respond to the
incoming light. Since the sensor array will most likely be read through multiplexers, the ESP32
firmware will scan the sensor groups, read the active sensor values, and compare those values
against the current global detection threshold.
The detection threshold is used to separate valid laser
hits from ambient light. If a sensor value does not pass
the current threshold, the software will ignore it. If one
or more sensors pass the threshold, the software will
treat them as possible shot candidates. This threshold
can be adjusted by the user through the physical controls
on the target or through the mobile app. This is
important because different rooms, lighting conditions,
and laser strengths may require different sensitivity
settings.

After the software finds the triggered sensors, it will
identify the strongest sensor response. This strongest
sensor acts as the main reference point for the shot. The
firmware will then look at nearby triggered sensors
around that point to determine whether the laser created
a small cluster of valid readings. This is expected
because the target may use a diffusion layer, and the
laser light may spread slightly across nearby sensors
instead of hitting only one exact sensor.

To estimate the final shot position, the software will use
a weighted average, also known as a weighted centroid.
Instead of treating every triggered sensor equally,
stronger sensor readings will have more influence on the
calculated position. This allows the software to use the
strongest sensor as the main point while still using
surrounding sensors to improve accuracy. The result is a
more accurate estimated shot location than simply
choosing one sensor by itself.

rejection. If a weak sensor is triggered far away from

If a second strong cluster appears far away from the first
cluster, the system may treat this as a multiple-shot event. In that case, the target can reject the

Once a valid shot location is calculated, the firmware will pass the result to the rest of the

Overall, the shot detection logic is designed to be accurate, adjustable, and realistic for the
hardware. The system does not simply check whether any sensor was triggered. Instead, it filters
the readings, finds the strongest response, checks nearby sensors, rejects outliers, detects possible
multiple shots, and calculates a weighted shot location that can be used for scoring and feedback.

4.2.4 Lighting Calibration Logic
Lighting calibration is needed because phototransistors can be affected by room lighting,
sunlight, reflections, and different laser strengths. If the detection threshold is too low, the system
may detect normal ambient light as a shot. If the threshold is too high, the system may miss a
real laser hit.

For the first prototype, the system will use one global detection threshold for the full sensor
array. Each sensor reading will be compared against this threshold. Readings below the threshold
will be ignored, while readings above the threshold will be treated as possible laser hits.

The threshold will be adjustable from the target using a rotary encoder or similar physical
control. The display will show the current calibration value so the user can tune the target for the
current lighting condition. The mobile app will also be able to adjust this value through
Bluetooth, possibly using simple presets such as dark room, normal room, bright room, and
custom.

The calibration value will apply immediately when changed. For the prototype, each sensor will
not have its own separate calibration value because that would add more firmware and storage
complexity. If testing shows that individual sensors behave very differently, per-sensor
calibration may be considered later.

4.2.5 Scoring and Accuracy Processing
Scoring and accuracy processing will happen after the software calculates the estimated shot
location. The scoring system will use the active training mode to decide how the shot should be
evaluated. Since each mode is designed for a different type of practice, the scoring rules may
change depending on whether the user is shooting in Freestyle, Classic, Rapid, or Moving Target
mode.

In Freestyle mode, the system will mainly show the user where the shot landed and what score
that shot would receive. This mode is meant for simple practice, so it does not need to heavily
track score history or a formal session total. In Classic mode, the target will use a traditional
center-based scoring system, where shots closer to the center receive higher values and shots
farther from the center receive lower values. A basic scoring range may use values from 10 down
to 1 based on the shot’s distance from the center.

Rapid mode and Moving Target mode may use smaller active scoring areas instead of the full
target. In these modes, the software may use a simpler scoring structure, such as higher points for
hitting the center of the active target area and fewer points for hitting near the edge. Rapid mode
may also consider how quickly the user hits the active target, while Moving Target mode may
focus on maintaining accuracy while the active target area changes position.

Accuracy processing will include more than just the raw score. The system may track the shot
location, distance from the center, number of shots fired, average score, and hit consistency. The
microcontroller will calculate the basic score needed for immediate feedback on the display.
When the mobile app is connected, the app can store shot records locally and calculate

4.2.7 Distance Tracking Logic
Distance tracking is used to record how far the user is standing from the target during training.
This is important because a shot at a close distance is not the same difficulty as a shot at a longer
distance. By saving distance with shot data, the system can give the user better context for their
scores, accuracy, and long-term performance.

For the first prototype, distance tracking will be handled manually instead of automatically. The
user will be able to enter or select the current shooting distance through the mobile app when the
app is connected. The user will also be able to set the distance directly on the target using the
onboard display and physical controls. This keeps the target fully usable without the app while
still allowing the app to make distance changes faster and easier from the user’s shooting
position.

The distance value will be stored as part of the system state. When a shot is detected, the current
distance setting will be included with the shot record. If the mobile app is connected, this
distance value will be sent with the shot data and saved in the app’s local history. If the app is not
connected, the target can still use the distance value for the display and local logging. The user
will also be able to choose the preferred unit, such as feet or meters, so the system can be used in
whatever measurement style is most comfortable.

Automatic distance tracking was considered as a possible future improvement, but it is not
planned for the first prototype. A camera-based system or AI-based distance detection would add
a large amount of extra complexity, including camera hardware, image processing, calibration,
and testing. Since the main goal of the project is to build a reliable laser target system, manual
distance entry is a more realistic choice for the current timeline.

In the future, the system could be expanded to support automatic distance measurement using a
camera, range sensor, or other external module. For now, manual distance entry gives the project
the needed distance data without making the software or hardware much harder than necessary.

Chapter 5: Prototype and Physical Design
5.1 Prototype Overview
When designing the physical layout of the PrecisionShot Training System, the primary focus
centered on creating a rugged, portable enclosure that protects internal electronics while ensuring
optimal performance from the light sensors. The physical design directly houses two of the three
main system pillars: the phototransistor input array and the control module. Since this is an
indoor training system meant to provide a cost-effective alternative to live-ammunition training,
the housing must be practical, durable, and structurally optimized to ensure clean data delivery to
the processing loop.

From an engineering perspective, balancing three main design priorities was critical to the
success of the prototype:
Ambient Light Isolation: Regular indoor lighting can flood the sensors, making it difficult for the
software's shot-detection logic to distinguish a true laser strike from background noise. To assist
the calibration firmware, the housing must naturally shade the sensors from the surrounding
environment. The design addresses this by incorporating a front-facing grid mesh directly over
the sensors. This mesh structure restricts light entry exclusively to direct, front-angled paths
while completely blocking ambient light bleed from the sides. The visible target pattern is
printed directly onto the face of this mesh for the user to aim at.

Structural Rigidity: The chassis must possess enough structural stiffness to completely resist
twisting, flexing, or warping. Any physical flexing within the frame shifts the coordinate
alignment of the sensor grid, which would directly compromise the spatial positioning accuracy
and score calculation logic within the software.

Easy Access to Components: The enclosure architecture incorporates a modular, serviceable rear
panel rather than a permanently sealed unit. This design provides direct access to internal
subsystems for hardware-in-the-loop debugging, test-point voltage validation, firmware flashing,
and routine battery maintenance without requiring a destructive or complex disassembly of the
target faceplate grid.

5.2 Target Layout
To optimize the interaction between the physical user interface and the optical detection
hardware, the internal area of the target chassis is divided into two primary functional zones. The
overall physical frame supports a main PCB footprint measuring 20 inches vertically by 14
inches horizontally. The lower section of this footprint allocates a 14-inch tall by 14-inch wide
region specifically to house the active target capture zone. The remaining upper 6 inches of the
chassis space is strictly dedicated to the control module, accommodating the user interface
buttons, routing paths, power management nodes, and the digital LCD display panel. This
vertical separation ensures that the interactive display components and control inputs remain
fully visible and shielded from accidental laser occlusion during active training sessions.
                         The active tracking region within the lower section of the chassis features
an elliptical target profile measuring 14 inches in height by 10 inches in width. While the
underlying mechanical frame maintains a complete 28-column by 20-row grid matrix, the 400
active phototransistor sensors are distributed non-uniformly across five distinct, concentric
scoring zones within this elliptical boundary. This adaptive density matrix places a higher
concentration of sensors near the center to ensure precision resolution where shots are most
tightly clustered, while gradually reducing sensor density toward the outer boundaries of the
ellipse.

The 400 active tracking nodes are systematically distributed across the five concentric zones
according to the following system breakdown:

Zone 1 (Bullseye): Positioned at the absolute geometric center of the active tracking region, this
inner ellipse core utilizes the highest density configuration, populating a core count of 146
sensors to capture precise high-accuracy shots.
Zone 2 (Inner Ring 1): Radiating immediately outward from the core bullseye, this zone features
a dense, staggered sensor layout utilizing a core count of 52 sensors.

Zone 3 (Inner Ring 2): Situated as the middle scoring band of the elliptical profile, this ring
implements a medium-density staggered layout containing a core count of 76 sensors.

Zone 4 (Outer Ring 1): Serving as the secondary outer scoring tier, this zone maintains a
medium-density perimeter layout populated by a core count of 66 sensors.

Zone 5 (Outer Rim): Defining the outermost scoring boundary of the 14-inch by 10-inch
elliptical target area, this region utilizes a lower-density perimeter layout with a core count of 60
sensors to detect edge impacts and off-center shots.

By mapping each individual sensor to a known coordinate pair within these defined boundaries,
the central firmware seamlessly translates real-time signal transitions into immediate shot
coordinates and zone-specific scores on the user interface display.

5.3 Physical Enclosure Design
The physical enclosure must protect the massive 20 in x 14 in internal board while remaining
easy to mount and transport.

The front faceplate acts as an optical filter for incoming laser light. The outer shield will be a 3D
printed acrylic case, matching the exact outer 20 in x 14 in boundaries of the internal PCB.

   ●​ Alignment Windows: Mechanical Sensor Support: Each aperture hole is designed with an
      internal diameter of 2.5 mm that perfectly aligns over the 0603 surface-mount footprint
      1.6 mm x 0.8 mm of the underlying ALS-PT204-6C/L177 phototransistors.
   ●​ Optical Isolation: The faceplate is printed with a uniform thickness of 4.0 mm. By
      recessing the flat, surface-mounted phototransistors behind these 4.0 mm deep
      mechanical tunnels, an angular shroud is created over each sensor node. This geometry
      limits the optical reception cone, physically blocking off-axis ambient room light
   ●​ Rear Vents: I made several 6 inch slits in the back of the enclosure to allow air flow
      inside the device to ensure it does not overheat

Because of the massive physical profile, the rear housing will be fabricated as a modular
structural frame. The frame will also be 3D printed using acrylic filament. Internal skeletal

To satisfy the optical isolation and structural durability requirements of the target assembly, the
physical enclosure integrates a custom front-facing grid plate. Shifting away from a standard
flat-panel layout, the target face features a dense, geometric matrix arranged in a 28 column by
20 row configuration, designed to maximize the active light-capture area. While the mechanical
grid contains 560 total cell cutouts to preserve structural symmetry across the panel, only the 400
active positions from the system layout are populated with phototransistor sensors. Each
individual cell within this matrix is constructed as a uniform square measuring 10.5 mm by 10.5
mm. Instead of utilizing straight vertical dividing walls, all four inner sides of every cell slope
inward at a precise 45 degree angle, forming a series of inverted square pyramids, or chamfered
funnels. This specific angle serves a dual purpose: it acts as a mechanical collector that channels
off-axis incoming laser light directly down toward the base of the cavity, and it allows the entire
component to be 3D printed cleanly without requiring temporary internal support structures.

At the absolute bottom and geometric center of each funnel rests a flat, 4.5 mm by 4.5 mm
aperture shelf. This opening aligns perfectly with the underlying printed circuit board, allowing
the optical lens of a centrally positioned phototransistor to peek through from the component
layer below. To provide immediate, localized user feedback upon a successful hit without
introducing optical interference, an indicator light emitting diode LED is integrated directly into
the upper sloped wall of the chamfered cavity. Rather than sitting exposed on the surface, the
LED is recessed inside a dedicated, isolated tunnel measuring 3.2 mm in diameter, tailored for a
standard 3mm package. This tunnel punches completely through the 45-degree sloped wall,
creating a physical barrier that directs the feedback illumination forward toward the shooter
while preventing any lateral or downward light leakage from blinding the adjacent
phototransistor sitting at the base.

Mechanically, the entire grid plate maintains a uniform total thickness of 5 mm along the Z-axis.
This depth is split to provide a 3 mm vertical drop for the chamfered collection zone and a robust
2 mm vertical base wall underneath. This structural thickness provides the necessary rigidity to
prevent the extensive multi-sensor panel from warping under thermal stress or mechanical
assembly pressures. To guarantee absolute optical isolation across all 400 active sensor nodes,
the grid plate is fabricated using an opaque, dark material. By ensuring a minimum solid wall
clearance of 1.3 mm between the perimeter of the LED tunnel and the outer face of the chamfer,

The upper 6.0-inch panel of the enclosure serves as the command console for the system,
housing the user controls and visual display. This section isolates high-voltage and
logic-switching noise away from the high-density sensor multiplexing arrays situated below.

The user interface is centered around an integrated 5.0-inch diagonal TFT LCD Module.

Mechanical Fit: The front faceplate features a clean, rectangular cutout measuring exactly 120.0
mm x 76.0, complete with an internal bezel lip. The display module is mounted from the rear,
flush against this lip, and secured to integrated mounting bosses using four M2.5 non-conductive
nylon screws.

Electrical Routing: To preserve the GPIO pins of the main ESP32 microcontroller, the display
utilizes an onboard controller backpack . This architecture collapses a massive 24-bit parallel
RGB ribbon interface down to a high-speed, 5-wire Serial Peripheral Interface bus, routing
cleanly to the ESP32 control board without interfering with the sensor matrix expanders.

Directly adjacent to the LCD screen, three tactile interface buttons are arrayed horizontally to
handle system parameters. Each button sits within a tolerated circular guide hole to prevent
binding during activation.

Mode Selection Button: A momentary tactile switch mapped to an ESP32 hardware external
interrupt line. It allows the user to cycle through firmware training modes.

Session Reset Button: Clears the active session variables, data arrays, shot counters, and
high-score logs from the ESP32’s internal RAM without needing a full system power cycle.

Ambient Calibration Button: Triggers an immediate analog-to-digital converter (ADC) baseline
scan across all 400 sensors. This allows the software to measure the room's current ambient light
levels and dynamically recalibrate the mathematical lookup thresholds.

5.5 Mounting and Assembly
Flawless alignment between the 400 phototransistors and the faceplate apertures is achieved by
surface-mounting the sensors directly to the top layer of our custom board. The board is then set
into place aligned with the faceplate using non-conductive nylon hex standoffs .

To accommodate different training environments, the target chassis features integrated
mechanical mounting points for both wall-mounted and tabletop deployment. The rear panel of
the enclosure includes a set of recessed, reinforced keyhole slots spaced to fit standard mounting
brackets or wall-anchor hardware. This design allows the entire target unit to hang securely on a
vertical surface while keeping the assembly flush against the wall to minimize movement when
struck. For flat-surface deployments, such as placing the target on a table or workbench, the
bottom face of the chassis is designed with a wide, stable footprint equipped with integrated slots
for non-slip rubber feet. This prevents the unit from shifting, tilting, or sliding on smooth
surfaces during operation.
Because the enclosure houses a large custom PCB alongside a heavy semi-solid-state power
bank, the interior of the chassis utilizes an array of molded structural supports to prevent internal
component flexing. The main sensor array PCB sits on top of a perimeter shelf equipped with
internal standoffs that elevate the board away from the outer walls, protecting the trace layers
and soldered joints from compression. The internal containment bracket for the power bank is
reinforced with vertical ribbing to handle the weight of the cells without warping over time. The
entire assembly is fastened together using a series of strategically placed, threaded screw points.
Rather than tapping screws directly into weak plastic threading, the chassis uses brass heat-set
inserts pressed into the internal standoffs. This ensures that the enclosure can withstand high
torque during assembly and allows for repeated disassembly without stripping the mounting
holes.

Maintaining absolute mechanical alignment between the front-facing grid plate and the
underlying sensor PCB is critical to prevent optical misalignment. To achieve this, the front bezel
incorporates internal alignment pins that mate directly with matching alignment holes on the
PCB. This mechanical constraint ensures that every 10.5 mm by 10.5 mm chamfered cell
remains dead-centered over its corresponding 4.5 mm by 4.5 mm sensor aperture shelf during the
final tightening phase. For routine maintenance or component troubleshooting, the enclosure is
designed for rear-panel access. The back faceplate functions as a removable hatch, secured by
recessed hex fasteners. Removing this panel exposes the back of the main PCB, the power bank
bracket, and the input routing cables. This structural layout ensures that the front grid faceplate
and optical paths remain completely sealed and undisturbed if internal electronics ever need to be
inspected, repaired, or replaced.

6.1 Budget Overview
The estimated budget for the PrecisionShot Training System is approximately $670 for the first
working prototype. This estimate includes the primary sensing hardware, PCB fabrication, user
interface components, enclosure materials, power system parts, wiring, connectors, and a small
allowance for shipping, taxes, and prototype mistakes. Since the project is still in the design and
testing stage, the final cost may change as components are selected, tested, replaced, or
redesigned.

The largest expected cost contributors are the phototransistor array, PCB fabrication, enclosure
materials, and prototype contingency budget. These areas are expected to drive the overall cost
because the system requires a large number of sensing points, a custom board layout, and a
physical housing that must align accurately with the sensor array. The design also includes
supporting hardware such as display components, control buttons, voltage regulation, signal
filtering, and rechargeable power components.

A budget margin is necessary because the design will likely change during prototyping. Early
hardware development often includes damaged parts, soldering mistakes, PCB revisions,
incorrect component selections, replacement sensors, or additional mounting hardware. Because
the PrecisionShot system depends on physical alignment between the enclosure, faceplate, and
phototransistor array, mechanical revisions may also increase cost. For this reason, the budget
includes a prototype mistake and shipping buffer rather than assuming every component will
work correctly on the first attempt.

Overall, the project remains reasonably affordable for a Senior Design prototype. The budget is
higher than the earliest estimate, but the updated amount better reflects a realistic first-build cost
for a custom sensor-based training system with a large phototransistor array, rechargeable power
system, display, and enclosure.

6.3 Cost Assumptions and Budget Risk
Several assumptions were made when preparing the prototype budget. First, the estimate
assumes that the team will build one complete working prototype rather than multiple full units.
Second, it assumes that some testing materials, tools, and basic lab supplies may already be
available through the university laboratory or team members. Third, it assumes that the first
prototype may require extra parts, replacement components, and minor redesigns before the
system works reliably.

The most significant budget risks are PCB revision costs, sensor quantity changes, enclosure
changes, and shipping delays. If the sensor array requires more phototransistors than expected,
the cost of the sensing system may increase. If the PCB layout requires a second fabrication run,
the total project cost may also increase. Similarly, if the enclosure requires multiple 3D printed
revisions to achieve proper sensor alignment, additional material and printing time may be
needed.
To control these risks, the team plans to test smaller sections of the system before committing to
the full final build. Breadboard testing, small-scale sensor validation, and early enclosure fit
checks will help reduce the chance of expensive mistakes during the final prototype stage.

6.4 Funding and Sponsorship
The current funding plan for the PrecisionShot Training System is for the team to pay for the
project out of pocket. With an estimated prototype cost of approximately $670, the cost is
expected to be manageable when divided among the four group members. This makes the project
financially realistic while still allowing the team to build a meaningful hardware and software
prototype.

Although the project can likely be self-funded, the team may still consider outside support if the
opportunity becomes available. Possible sources of support include university resources,
department assistance, faculty guidance, donated materials, or individuals interested in dry-fire
training, firearm safety, or low-cost training tools. Sponsorship is not required for the project to
continue, but additional support could allow the team to purchase better components, reduce
personal costs, or improve the final prototype quality.

At this stage, the project budget is focused on completing a functional prototype rather than
producing a polished commercial product. If additional funding becomes available, priority
would be given to improving the PCB quality, enclosure durability, sensor reliability, and overall
user experience.

6.5 Estimated Mass Production Cost and Margins
While the current budget is based on a single working prototype, the projected cost of the
PrecisionShot Training System would decrease significantly if the product were produced in
larger quantities. The prototype budget includes one-time costs, small-quantity component
pricing, shipping fees, replacement parts, and design mistakes. In mass production, many of
these costs would be reduced through bulk ordering, panelized PCB fabrication, automated
assembly, and standardized enclosure manufacturing.

For a larger production batch of approximately 1,000 units or more, the estimated manufacturing
cost per unit could potentially decrease to about $110 to $170. This estimate assumes that
phototransistors, passive components, microcontrollers, display modules, buttons, connectors,
battery components, and power hardware are purchased in bulk. PCB fabrication and assembly
would also become more cost-efficient because the board could be manufactured in panels and
assembled using automated pick-and-place equipment.

The enclosure manufacturing method would have a major impact on cost. For the prototype, 3D
printing is useful because it avoids tooling costs and allows the design to be changed quickly.
However, 3D printing is slower and more expensive per unit, especially for a housing large
enough to protect the sensor array and control electronics. A 3D printed enclosure may cost
approximately $25 to $60 per unit, depending on size, material, print time, and finishing.

For mass production, injection-molded plastic would likely become the better option once the
design is finalized. Plastic molding would require a much higher initial tooling cost, potentially
several thousand dollars or more, but the per-unit enclosure cost could drop to approximately $5
to $15 once enough units are produced. This would make the product easier to assemble
consistently and would improve the appearance, durability, and repeatability of the enclosure.

A realistic retail price for the PrecisionShot system would likely fall between $599 and $799.
This price is higher than the raw manufacturing cost because selling a real product requires more
than paying for parts and assembly. The final sale price would need to account for research and
development, engineering redesigns, testing, packaging, documentation, warranty replacements,
customer support, marketing, online advertising, platform fees, payment processing, inventory
storage, shipping logistics, product photography, listing management, and possible Amazon
marketplace costs.

The higher retail price also helps recover costs that are not included in the physical bill of
materials. Even if the system could eventually be manufactured for around $110 to $170 per unit,
the company would still need to recover the time and money spent on product design, firmware
development, mobile app development, PCB revisions, enclosure revisions, safety testing, failed
prototypes, and early marketing. These costs are normally spread across many units, which is
why the final retail price is often several times higher than the manufacturing cost.

If the system were sold for approximately $599 to $799, the gross margin could be strong
enough to support continued development, advertising, warranty service, and future product
improvements. However, the actual net profit would be lower after seller fees, fulfillment costs,
returned units, customer support, advertising campaigns, taxes, and other business expenses are
removed. Therefore, the estimated retail price should be viewed as a practical commercial target
rather than a simple markup on parts.

These estimates are preliminary and are not intended to represent a final business plan. They are
included to show that the project has a possible path from prototype to mass production. The
team’s main priority remains building and validating a functional Senior Design prototype. Any
future commercialization would require a finalized bill of materials, supplier quotes, safety
review, manufacturing design revisions, enclosure tooling analysis, fulfillment planning, and
market comparison against existing laser training products.
